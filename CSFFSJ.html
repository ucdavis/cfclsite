Fathers & Families of San Joaquin

Topic:

Comprehensive Community Development (youth/elder needs, trauma-informed healing, juvenile justice, educational equity, environmental justice)

Timeline:

2001-Present

Home Organization:

Fathers & Families of San Joaquin

Location:

Stockton, CA

Youth In Focus had the privilege of working with Fathers & Families of San Joaquin (FFSJ) early on in its development, supporting a needs assessment that helped launch the organization’s work. This partnership has continued into the present day, with FFSJ facilitating YPAR projects in collaboration with staff from the UC Davis Center for Regional Change and its Putting Youth on the Map Program, amongst others. Over the past two decades, FFSJ has embedded YPAR within its practice, integrating it into their healing justice work with formerly incarcerated and system-impacted youth and families as well as efforts to promote educational equity and environmental justice. The following is FFSJ’s story as told by the organization’s founder and Executive Director, Samuel “Sammy” Nuñez.

When you are incarcerated you are seen as less than human. Oppression has an order. Lies, misinformation and half-truths are repeated until people start to believe them, even the target population. If you are bad you can never be good. If you are bad you deserve nothing but a cage. Messages turn into policies and lead to destabilization. Fear-based reality divides us from each other. It creates imbalance.

I didn’t have a plan. I just wanted to do something positive for my neighborhood and people. We were suffering. I was suffering. I wanted to know how do you stop the pain? And so I stepped in. My experience told me that if we didn’t do it no one else would.

When we started we had no money, no office, we only had the parks. We wanted to wake up the people and serve them with dignity. We gathered young fathers and started meeting as a group in the public park. We weren’t looking for an organization, we were just looking for transformation.

Data can be weaponized and used against us to create a misdiagnosis. We knew the diagnosis was wrong. The treatment plan was a punishment plan and it was causing more harm. If you know something is causing harm and do nothing to prevent it you are also complicit. We needed to examine the problem for ourselves.

We went through YPAR training and learned how to do research and break down data. The data started telling us a story about how the violence began, why it continued and how it was rooted in the state through policies and practices that targeted our people. Then we could see the linkages by connecting the data with our lived experiences.

When you become masterful at YPAR and you have the experiences that can add depth and substance to the data, it gives numbers spirit, heart, soul. You can feel it. This is really about telling a story of what we already knew. Then we can attack this issue rather than react to it.

In 2003, we became part of a coalition called ESPINO [Escuelas Sí, Pintas No (Schools Yes, Prisons No)]. This brought our work to another level. We began to radically imagine a different world. We saw we needed a language of love that would penetrate through hate, despair, fear. We decided to target the school-to-prison pipeline and realized we were the hope we had been waiting for. Nothing about us without us is for us.

We couldn’t breathe long before COVID-19 decimated our community. Stockton knew what it was like to live amid an epidemic. The epidemic of a war that targeted our brown and black communities. The epidemic of a mortgage crisis and years of gentrification that pushes hard- working families out of their homes. The epidemic of an economy that relies on our labor but denies working people dignity and respect. An epidemic of trauma, illiteracy, and violence caused by cycles of poverty which leave people hurting, hopeless, and with few opportunities for change.

At the time Stockton was in bankruptcy, with racial toxic inequity and policies that placed burdens that people had to shoulder instead of removing the burdens that weighed in their hearts. The Central Valley was seeing the biggest prison expansion in our history. It is a dark shameful time in California. Prisons were promised to be a resource engine to the regional economy and they were making us sicker.

In order to understand something you have to understand how it is connected to everything. That was an epiphany. We looked at data and trends, forecasts and algorithms. Prisons were not the resource engine that they were professed to be. We had data to prove it. We used YPAR to see this and think about systems that would be better. It also exposed the root problem: white supremacy.

Loving yourself in a society that teaches you to hate yourself is a revolutionary act. We saw the generational impact of compounded racial inequity. The actual burdens and barriers of structural racism. You can’t understand individual behavior without seeing that. Once you understand it you don’t fear it. You control your own destiny. Nothing could stop us.

The State of California has a debt to pay to Stockton and the Central Valley. The State needs to see our wholeness and invest in our fullness to make good on that promise. Stockton will move this state—as Stockton goes, so goes the Valley; and as the Valley goes, so goes the State.

We launched a campaign and started doing direct action and publishing our own reports. We wrote the State of the Hood to talk about the state of childhood, parenthood, neighborhoods in the Valley using data plus our own experiences. Our fundamental agenda was healing. Healing the wounds of generational trauma to enable reconciliation. You can’t move a healing agenda through a punishment model. It has to be transformed.

A proper diagnosis is half the cure and the key to survival. The problem isn’t our people. We know that all life is sacred, everyone has intrinsic worth and together we can be instruments of peace.

The problem is that for decades, California’s policies and investments have treated poverty and trauma with solutions that don’t work—policing and incarceration, distrust and disinvestment. It’s made us sicker. And we are sick of being sick and tired of being tired. We have now taken matters into our own hands. We are at the epicenter where the impact is located; where the point of impact is concentrated. We believe that people closest to the problems are closest to the solutions to change the trajectory and chart a new course.

We started doing work to bring mamas and papas home, heal them up and then activate them. Our world view was shaped by the past. We needed to see what could be. We needed to take this data and create a new reality. These are the origins of our agency and the genetic code of our foundation. Our DNA is pain, shame, power, harmony, and healing.

The gang injunctions had fueled land grabs throughout the state. We created a movement in Stockton to reclaim this land. We created a youth and family empowerment zone. It includes a healing center, youth empowerment center, and an elder center. We started to provide access to traditional healing ceremonies, sweat lodges, healing circles, and naming ceremonies. These ceremonies gave us the cohesion and spiritual fortitude to face these monsters together. This became the basis of our work. We needed to heal, deal, and keep it real.

Our healing strategy has 4 tenets. Healing is political; Healing and Organizing intersect; Healing responds to the needs of the community; and Healing is rooted in our spirituality and cultural traditions.

We are an anchor in the region and have been putting Stockton on the map. We are the most diverse city in America, and the most segregated city in America. We are using our infrastructure and decolonizing data to reshape the built environment, redefine public safety, and renew the spirit of our people toward imagining a new future. The future can heal the past. We began a new lineage with new memories, new hopes, and new visions not grounded in suffering and scarcity.

Our next phase is Stockton Rooted. Stockton Reimagined. Stockton Reclaimed. By building BLOCS (Building Love, Opportunity & Connectivity in Stockton). They don’t want us on their blocks, so we have to build our own by reclaiming the land and re-rooting our community. There is a crisis of capitalism in our state. You see it over and over and over. People are being pushed away and out. We want to acquire land so that at least more than 20% of Stocktonians own land. Prisons are closing in our area and we want to repurpose this land and grow through it. We want to create gardens, housing, healing centers. We want to turn the state-of-the-art prison hospital into a community hospital. These are the things we need to replenish and renew. It’s critical.

In the long run we want to build capacity to solve our own problems. In 20 years, we should all still be here. The children who are growing up now should still be here, raising families, with their parents as grandparents, growing gardens, harmony and healing.

As we continue to see massive gentrification from the Bay Area, regional inequity is stark and real. Our people have long known what it takes to survive a pandemic: hope, healing, and love. Stockton is the centerpiece of the Civil Rights Movement of our generation.

The moment is right now. The question is what kind of ancestors will we be? We will be defined by the investments and choices we make today. Let us live to our espoused values as the Golden State.
